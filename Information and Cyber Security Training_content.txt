Slide 3: Understanding the Basics: InfoSec & CyberSec Concepts
Information Security (InfoSec)
Definition: InfoSec is the overarching practice of protecting information from unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction. It applies to information in all its forms – digital, physical (paper documents), and even verbal discussions.
Focus: It's a holistic approach, encompassing people, processes, and technology to ensure the Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (CIA Triad) of all our information assets.
Pillars of InfoSec (The CIA Triad) - Our Core Principles:
Confidentiality: This means ensuring that information is accessible only to those authorized to view it.
Think: Keeping secrets secret.
Examples: Encrypting sensitive files, implementing strict access controls (who can view what), shredding confidential documents, not discussing sensitive matters in public places.
Integrity: This means maintaining the accuracy, completeness, and consistency of data throughout its entire lifecycle. Data should not be altered without authorization.
Think: Keeping information trustworthy and uncorrupted.
Examples: Using data validation checks, ensuring file checksums match, controlling who can modify records, having version control for critical documents.
Availability: This means ensuring that authorized users have timely and uninterrupted access to information and systems when needed.
Think: Keeping systems up and running.
Examples: Regular data backups, implementing disaster recovery plans, ensuring robust network infrastructure, protecting against denial-of-service attacks.
Cybersecurity
Definition: Cybersecurity is a subset of Information Security, specifically focused on protecting computer systems, networks, and digital data from digital attacks, damage, or unauthorized access.
Focus: It involves specific technical controls, tools, processes, and human practices to defend our digital environment.
Key Areas: Network security (firewalls, VPNs), application security (secure coding), endpoint security (antivirus, device encryption), cloud security, data security (encryption, DLP), and incident response.
Threat, Vulnerability, and Risk - Understanding the Equation
Threat: A potential danger that could exploit a vulnerability to breach security and cause harm.
Examples: A cybercriminal, a malicious insider, a piece of malware, a natural disaster (e.g., flood causing server damage).
Vulnerability: A weakness or flaw in a system, process, or design that could be exploited by a threat.
Examples: Unpatched software, a weak password, lack of encryption on a sensitive file, a lax policy, an untrained employee.
Risk: The potential for loss, damage, or destruction of an asset as a result of a threat exploiting a vulnerability.
Formula: Risk = Threat x Vulnerability x Asset Value.
Our Goal: To identify, assess, and mitigate these risks to an acceptable level through robust security measures and vigilance.
Slide 4: The Cyber Threat Landscape in India
Key Trends & Statistics (Indian Context) - A Growing Challenge
Increased Digitalization: India's rapid adoption of digital payments, e-governance, cloud services, and mobile-first strategies creates a massive attack surface. More digital interactions mean more targets for cybercriminals.
Phishing & Social Engineering: These remain the number one attack vectors globally and in India. Attackers exploit human vulnerabilities, not just technical ones.
Ransomware: A highly disruptive and growing threat. Ransomware attacks cripple businesses by encrypting data and demanding high payments (often in cryptocurrency) to restore access. It's a direct threat to our business continuity.
Insider Threats: Whether accidental (e.g., falling for a phishing scam) or malicious (e.g., disgruntled employee), actions by internal personnel can lead to significant data breaches or system compromise.
Targeting Critical Infrastructure: Attacks on power grids, financial systems, telecommunications, and healthcare systems are a serious concern, often with geopolitical motivations.
Rise in Data Breaches: Indian companies are frequently featured in global data breach reports, highlighting the need for stronger defenses.
Geopolitical Factors: State-sponsored cyber attacks and espionage targeting Indian entities are a persistent, sophisticated threat.
Mobile Threats: The widespread use of mobile devices for work introduces new vulnerabilities through insecure apps, public Wi-Fi, and lost/stolen devices.
Common Targets in India - Why Us?
Cybercriminals and malicious actors prioritize targets based on data value, financial gain, and potential disruption. In India, common targets include:
Financial Services: Banks, FinTech companies, payment gateways, and stockbrokers are prime targets for direct financial fraud, data theft, and disruption due to the high value of financial transactions and personal data.
IT & ITES (Information Technology Enabled Services): Due to large data volumes, global client bases, and access to sensitive client data, they face sophisticated and persistent attacks, often aimed at supply chain compromise.
Healthcare: Hospitals, clinics, and health-tech companies hold highly sensitive patient data, making them attractive for ransomware, data exfiltration, and identity theft.
Government & Public Sector: Critical infrastructure, vast citizen data, and national security interests make these high-value targets for both financially motivated and state-sponsored attacks.
Manufacturing & Automotive: With the rise of Industry 4.0 and interconnected operational technology (OT) systems, these sectors face risks of intellectual property theft, industrial espionage, and operational disruption.
Small & Medium Enterprises (SMEs): Often have weaker security postures due to limited resources, making them easier targets for opportunistic attackers who then might use them as a stepping stone to larger partners.
Why Are Indian Organizations Targeted? - Our Vulnerabilities
Growing Digital Economy: The rapid growth makes India a lucrative target for cybercriminals seeking financial gain.
Legacy Systems: Many organizations still rely on older, unpatched systems with known vulnerabilities, creating easy entry points.
Lack of Awareness: Insufficient cybersecurity training and awareness among employees remains a significant vulnerability across sectors.
Resource Constraints: Smaller organizations may lack adequate budgets and skilled personnel for robust security programs.
Supply Chain Vulnerabilities: Weaknesses in third-party vendors, suppliers, and partners can inadvertently expose larger, more secure organizations to risk.
Slide 5: Indian Legal & Regulatory Framework for Cybersecurity
Understanding our legal obligations is fundamental to our cybersecurity strategy. Non-compliance can lead to severe penalties.
1. Information Technology Act, 2000 (IT Act) & Amendments
The Primary Law: This is India's foundational legislation governing cybercrime, electronic commerce, and data protection. It provides the legal framework for addressing digital activities.
Key Provisions (Relevant to Cybersecurity - Know These!):
Section 43 (Penalty for damage to computer etc.): Imposes penalties for unauthorized access, data theft, denial of service, data alteration, and other damages to computer systems/data.
Actionable: Don't engage in any unauthorized access or activities, even out of curiosity.
Section 66 (Computer related offences): Defines and penalizes various cybercrimes, including hacking, data theft, identity theft, cheating by impersonation using computer resources.
Actionable: Be aware of these crimes and how your actions might inadvertently enable them.
Section 66B (Punishment for dishonestly receiving stolen computer resource or communication device): Penalizes receiving a stolen computer or communication device.
Section 66F (Punishment for cyber terrorism): Imposes severe penalties for acts like unauthorized access to critical information infrastructure with intent to cause harm, or disrupting computer networks.
Section 72 (Penalty for breach of confidentiality and privacy): Penalizes anyone who, having secured access to electronic record, document, or information by virtue of the IT Act, discloses it without consent or lawful authority.
Actionable: Strict adherence to data privacy and confidentiality.
Section 79 (Exemption from liability of intermediary in certain cases): Defines the role of intermediaries (e.g., social media platforms, ISPs, cloud providers) and outlines conditions under which they may be held liable for content or activities on their platforms.
Section 85 (Offences by companies): States that if an offense under the IT Act is committed by a company, the person in charge and responsible for the conduct of the business, as well as the company itself, shall be liable, unless due diligence is proven.
Implication: This means individuals (like you!) can be held personally accountable if they fail in their duties.
Information Technology (Reasonable Security Practices and Procedures and Sensitive Personal Data or Information) Rules, 2011 (IT Rules):
Mandate: These rules specify "reasonable security practices" that entities collecting, storing, or processing "Sensitive Personal Data or Information (SPDI)" must follow.
SPDI Categories: Include passwords, financial information (bank account, credit/debit card details), health conditions, sexual orientation, biometric information, and any other information relating to the individual that is received, stored, or processed.
Key Requirements: These rules mandate a comprehensive documented privacy policy, explicit consent for SPDI collection, purpose limitation, disclosure controls, and a grievance redressal mechanism.
Actionable: Our company's internal data handling policies are designed to comply with these rules. You must follow them diligently, especially when handling SPDI.
2. Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (DPDP Act)
India's New Data Protection Law: This is a landmark, comprehensive law specifically governing the processing of digital personal data in India. It will strengthen privacy rights and impose stricter obligations.
Key Relevance to Cybersecurity:
Section 8(5) (Obligations of Data Fiduciary): Explicitly mandates Data Fiduciaries (our company) to implement reasonable security safeguards to prevent personal data breaches. This is a direct, legally binding cybersecurity obligation.
Actionable: Our investment in security infrastructure, software, and this very training is directly linked to this obligation. Your adherence to security protocols directly contributes to fulfilling this.
Section 8(6) (Obligation to notify data breach): Requires Data Fiduciaries to notify the Data Protection Board of India and affected Data Principals (individuals) in case of a personal data breach. Rules will specify the timeline and manner of notification.
Implication: Rapid incident reporting (as discussed in Slide 10) is crucial for us to meet these notification deadlines.
Penalties: Imposes significant penalties for failure to protect data (up to INR 250 Crore for security safeguard failures).
Impact: This underscores the financial and reputational risk of a data breach stemming from inadequate security.
Implication: The DPDP Act significantly elevates the importance of robust cybersecurity measures for any organization processing digital personal data in India. It moves beyond just preventing hacking to actively protecting personal data.
3. Cybersecurity Policies & Guidelines (CERT-In)
Indian Computer Emergency Response Team (CERT-In): This is the national nodal agency for cybersecurity incident response in India. They are our go-to authority for guidance and alerts.
Role: CERT-In issues crucial advisories, alerts, and guidelines on cybersecurity incidents, emerging vulnerabilities, and best practices. They act as the central point for reporting and coordinating responses to cyber incidents in India.
Key Directives (Actionable!): CERT-In has issued various directives that directly impact our operations, most notably:
Mandatory Reporting of Cybersecurity Incidents: Requires all service providers, data centers, government organizations, and corporate bodies to mandatorily report cybersecurity incidents to CERT-In within 6 hours of noticing or being brought to notice of such incidents.
This includes: Data breaches, system intrusions, ransomware attacks, unauthorized access, identity theft, and more.
Maintenance of ICT Logs: Requires organizations to store and maintain their Information and Communication Technology (ICT) logs for a rolling period of 180 days in India.
Impact: Our company must strictly adhere to CERT-In's directives and reporting requirements. Your prompt incident reporting directly feeds into our ability to comply with these mandatory timelines.
4. Sector-Specific Regulations
Beyond national laws, many industries have their own specific cybersecurity regulations:
Reserve Bank of India (RBI): Extensive guidelines for banks, financial institutions, and payment system operators on IT governance, cyber security frameworks, and incident reporting (e.g., RBI Circular on 'Cyber Security Framework for Banks', 'Master Direction on Digital Payment Security Controls').
Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI): Cybersecurity and cyber resilience frameworks for market infrastructure institutions (e.g., stock exchanges) and other regulated entities.
Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India (IRDAI): Guidelines for insurers on information and cybersecurity management.
And many more...

Slide 6: Common Cyber Threats - What We Face Every Day
Understanding the enemy helps us defend better. Here are the most common threats you'll encounter:
1. Malware (Malicious Software)
Definition: Any software specifically designed to cause damage to a computer, server, or network system, or gain unauthorized access.
Types (Be Aware!):
Viruses: Attach to legitimate programs (like an infected file or application) and spread when those programs are executed.
Worms: Self-replicating malware that spreads autonomously across networks without needing a host program.
Trojan Horses: Masquerade as legitimate software (e.g., a "free game" or "useful utility") to trick users into installing them, then provide backdoors for attackers.
Spyware: Secretly monitors user activity (e.g., keystrokes, browsing history, screenshots) and collects sensitive information without consent.
Adware: Displays unwanted advertisements, often bundled with free software.
Rootkits: Stealthy tools that enable unauthorized access to a computer and hide their presence, often by modifying operating system processes.
How it Spreads (Your Action!):
Malicious Email Attachments: The most common vector.
Infected Websites: Visiting compromised or malicious websites.
Compromised Downloads: Downloading software from unofficial sources.
USB Drives/External Media: Plugging in untrusted USB sticks.
2. Phishing & Social Engineering - The Human Element Attack
Phishing: Deceptive attempts to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information (like passwords, bank details) or installing malware by impersonating a trustworthy entity (e.g., a bank, IT department, senior management) in electronic communication (email, SMS).
Spear Phishing: Highly targeted phishing attacks against specific individuals or organizations, often using personalized information.
Whaling: Even more targeted phishing against senior executives or high-profile individuals ("the big fish").
Smishing: Phishing via SMS (text messages).
Vishing: Phishing via voice calls.
Social Engineering: The art of manipulating people into performing actions or divulging confidential information. It exploits human psychology, trust, curiosity, fear, or a sense of urgency. It's often the gateway to other attacks.
Pretexting: Creating a fabricated scenario (a "pretext") to extract information (e.g., pretending to be IT support to get your login).
Baiting: Offering something enticing (e.g., a "free movie download," an infected USB left in a parking lot) to lure victims into a trap.
Quid Pro Quo: Promising a benefit (e.g., "I'll fix your internet if you give me your password") in exchange for information or action.
Your Action!: BE SUSPICIOUS! Verify, verify, verify. This is your most critical defense. (More on this on Slide 9).
3. Ransomware - The Business Crippler
Definition: A particularly nasty type of malware that encrypts a victim's files or locks their entire computer system, then demands a ransom payment (usually in hard-to-trace cryptocurrency like Bitcoin) to restore access.
Impact: Can entirely halt business operations, lead to massive financial loss (both ransom and recovery costs), and result in permanent data loss if robust backups are not available or are also encrypted.
How it Spreads (Your Action!): Primarily through phishing emails, malicious websites, or exploiting unpatched software vulnerabilities.
4. Denial-of-Service (DoS) / Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) - System Shutdown
Definition: Attacks designed to make a machine or network resource unavailable to its intended users by overwhelming it with traffic or disrupting its services.
DDoS: A DoS attack launched from multiple compromised computer systems (a "botnet") simultaneously, making it harder to block.
Impact: Website downtime, interruption of critical online services, significant reputational damage, and financial losses due to lost business.
5. Insider Threats - The Risk from Within
Definition: Security risks that originate from within the organization, often from current or former employees, contractors, or business partners who have legitimate access.
Types:
Malicious Insider: Deliberately steals data, sabotages systems, or causes harm due to intent (e.g., a disgruntled employee, an employee leaving to join a competitor).
Negligent Insider: Unintentionally causes a security incident due to carelessness, poor security practices, or falling for social engineering (e.g., clicking a phishing link, losing a company laptop, sharing credentials). This is far more common!
Impact: Data breaches, intellectual property theft, system sabotage, unauthorized disclosure of confidential information.
6. Zero-Day Exploits - The Unknown Vulnerability
Definition: An attack that exploits a previously unknown software vulnerability for which no patch or fix exists from the vendor.
Risk: Extremely dangerous as security teams have no prior knowledge or available defenses to protect against them until the vulnerability is discovered and a patch is released.
Your Action!: While difficult to defend against directly, robust general security practices (least privilege, network segmentation) help limit their impact.
Slide 7: Data Security Best Practices - Protecting Our Information
These practices ensure our data remains Confidential, maintains Integrity, and stays Available.
1. Data Classification - Knowing Your Data
Concept: Categorizing data based on its sensitivity, value, and regulatory requirements. This helps us apply the right level of protection.
Our Classifications (Example):
Public: Information intended for general public consumption (e.g., marketing material).
Internal: Company information not intended for public release, but generally shared within the company (e.g., internal memos).
Confidential: Sensitive business information, intellectual property, or non-public financial data (e.g., product designs, strategic plans, unreleased earnings).
Restricted/Highly Confidential: Most sensitive data, whose unauthorized disclosure would cause severe damage (e.g., customer PII/SPDI, employee records, critical financial data, trade secrets). This requires the highest level of protection.
Importance: Knowing a document's classification helps you understand how to handle it: where it can be stored, who it can be shared with, and how it should be disposed of.
2. Access Control (Least Privilege Principle) - Who Sees What
Principle: Users should only have access to the data and systems absolutely necessary to perform their specific job functions, and nothing more. This limits the "blast radius" if an account is compromised.
Practices:
Regularly Review: Access rights are reviewed periodically to ensure they are still appropriate for current roles.
Prompt Removal/Modification: Access is removed promptly when an employee's role changes or they leave the company.
Role-Based Access: Access is granted based on your job role, not individual requests.
Strong Authentication: Always use your unique login credentials and Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).
3. Strong Password & Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) - Your Digital Keys
Passwords (Your FIRST Line of Defense):
Long & Complex: Aim for at least 12-16 characters. Combine uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols.
Unique: Never reuse passwords across different accounts (especially between personal and work accounts).
Memorize (Don't Write Down!): If you must, use a secure password manager.
Never Share: Your password is yours alone. Our IT team will NEVER ask for your password.
Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) - Your SECOND Layer of Defense:
Adds an extra layer of security beyond just a password (e.g., a code from an authenticator app, a fingerprint scan, a one-time password via SMS to your registered mobile).
Always enable MFA wherever possible for all corporate accounts (email, VPN, internal systems) and for your critical personal accounts. It drastically reduces the risk of account compromise.
4. Data Encryption - Speaking in Code
Concept: Converting data into a coded, unreadable format that can only be deciphered with the correct key. This protects data even if unauthorized access occurs.
Where We Encrypt (and You Should Too!):
Data at Rest: Encrypt data stored on hard drives (your laptop, desktop, servers), cloud storage, and databases.
Actionable: Ensure your company-issued laptop's hard drive is encrypted (check with IT if unsure).
Data in Transit: Encrypt data as it travels across networks (e.g., using HTTPS for websites, VPNs for remote access, secure file transfer protocols).
Actionable: Always use company-approved secure channels for transferring sensitive files.
Company Policy: Our company encrypts all sensitive data at rest and in transit using industry-standard protocols.
5. Data Backup & Recovery - Our Safety Net
Importance: Critical for recovering from ransomware attacks, accidental deletion, system failures, or natural disasters. Backups ensure Availability.
Best Practices (The 3-2-1 Rule for Critical Data):
3 copies of your critical data (original + two backups).
On 2 different types of storage media (e.g., internal hard drive, network drive, cloud).
1 copy stored off-site (e.g., in a secure cloud backup separate from your primary data center, or a physically separate location).
Regular Testing: Backups must be regularly tested to ensure they are recoverable and uncorrupted.
Your Action!: Ensure your critical work documents are saved on designated network drives or cloud storage solutions that are part of the company's backup strategy, NOT just on your local desktop.
6. Secure Data Disposal - Leaving No Traces
Concept: Properly and securely disposing of data, both digital and physical, so it cannot be recovered or accessed by unauthorized individuals. Simple deletion is NOT enough for digital data.
Digital Data:
Hard Drives/SSDs: Use data wiping or degaussing (demagnetizing) software that overwrites data multiple times.
Mobile Devices: Perform a factory reset and ensure data wiping.
Company Devices: Never dispose of company devices yourself. Return them to IT for proper secure disposal.
Physical Data:
Confidential Documents: Always shred documents containing confidential, sensitive, or personal information. Do not just throw them in the trash.
Storage Media: Physically destroy CDs, DVDs, USB drives that contain sensitive information.
Your Action!: Follow company guidelines for disposing of physical and digital records. When in doubt, shred it or ask IT.
Slide 8: Network & Endpoint Security - Our Digital Fortifications
These are the technical safeguards protecting our digital perimeter and individual devices.
Network Security - Protecting Our Digital Headquarters
Firewalls: Think of firewalls as the security guards at our network's gates. They act as a barrier between our internal network and external networks (like the internet), controlling incoming and outgoing network traffic based on predefined security rules. They block unauthorized access attempts.
Intrusion Detection/Prevention Systems (IDS/IPS):
IDS: Monitors network traffic for suspicious activity and alerts our security administrators. It's like a silent alarm system.
IPS: Actively blocks or prevents detected threats from entering the network or spreading within it. It's like the security guard who immediately apprehends the intruder.
Virtual Private Networks (VPNs):
Creates a secure, encrypted "tunnel" connection over a public network (like the internet) to our company network.
Mandatory for Remote Access: When working from home, a café, or traveling, connecting to our company network via VPN ensures your data in transit is protected from eavesdropping.
Your Action!: Always use the company-provided VPN when accessing internal resources remotely. Do not use unsecured public Wi-Fi without a VPN.
Secure Wi-Fi Practices:
Our company Wi-Fi uses strong encryption (WPA2/WPA3) and complex passwords.
Your Action!:
Avoid connecting to unsecured public Wi-Fi networks (e.g., in cafes, airports) for company work unless using the company-approved VPN.
Be cautious about "free Wi-Fi" hotspots, as they can be fake and used to steal data.
Endpoint Security - Protecting Your Workstation
Definition: Protecting individual computing devices (endpoints) such as your company laptop, desktop, mobile phone, and servers from cyber threats. These are your personal digital workspaces.
Antivirus & Anti-Malware Software:
Mandatory: This software is installed on all company-issued devices. It detects, prevents, and removes malicious software.
Your Action!:
Keep it updated: Ensure your antivirus definition files are current (often automatic, but verify).
Enable real-time scanning: Make sure the software is actively monitoring your system.
Perform regular full system scans: As advised by IT.
Never disable your antivirus software.
Patch Management (Keeping Software Up-to-Date):
Concept: Regularly updating operating systems (Windows, macOS), applications (browsers, office suites), and firmware with the latest security patches released by vendors.
Why it's Critical: Patches fix known vulnerabilities that attackers frequently exploit. Unpatched software is an open door for cybercriminals.
Your Action!:
Do not delay updates on company-issued devices. Allow updates to run as scheduled.
Report any issues encountered during updates to IT.
Device Security (Physical & Digital):
Physical Security:
Keep devices locked: Never leave your laptop or mobile phone unattended in public places.
Secure your workspace: Lock your office desk/cabin.
Use cable locks where provided.
Encryption:
Ensure your device hard drives are encrypted (e.g., BitLocker for Windows, FileVault for macOS). This protects data if the device is lost or stolen.
Your Action!: Confirm with IT that your device is encrypted.
Password/PIN/Biometrics: Enable strong authentication (PIN, password, fingerprint, facial recognition) for device unlock.
Lost/Stolen Devices: Report immediately to IT so devices can be remotely wiped or locked, preventing data compromise.
Software Installation Policy:
Your Action!:
Only install company-approved software from official sources (e.g., our internal software distribution portal).
Avoid installing unverified third-party applications, games, or browser extensions. These can contain malware or create vulnerabilities.
Be cautious about granting excessive permissions to apps, especially on mobile devices.
Slide 9: The Human Element: Social Engineering & Your Vigilance
YOU are our strongest defense, or our weakest link. Let's make you our strongest!
Social Engineering Revisited: Why Attackers Target YOU
Cybercriminals often target humans because it's generally easier than breaking through robust technical defenses like firewalls or encryption.
They exploit fundamental human traits:
Trust: Impersonating someone you know or trust (IT, HR, senior manager).
Curiosity: "Click this link to see who viewed your profile!"
Fear/Urgency: "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours if you don't act now!"
Greed: "Congratulations, you've won a lottery! Click here to claim your prize."
Authority: Impersonating a CEO demanding immediate action.
Remember: No technical control can fully protect against a determined social engineer if you are not vigilant. You are the "Human Firewall."
Common Social Engineering Tactics - Watch Out for These!
Phishing Emails/Messages (The Most Common!):
Urgent, Demanding Language: "Immediate action required!", "Your account will be suspended!"
Requests for Sensitive Information: Asking for your login credentials, bank details, Aadhaar/PAN, or OTPs.
Impersonation: Email appears to be from a colleague, manager, IT, or a known vendor, but the email address is slightly off (e.g., ceo@ourcomany.com vs. ceo@oourcompany.com).
Unexpected Attachments/Links: An email with an attachment from an unknown sender, or a link to a suspicious website.
Grammatical Errors, Spelling Mistakes, Awkward Phrasing: Often a strong indicator of a non-legitimate source.
Generic Greetings: "Dear Customer" instead of your name.
Suspicious URLs: Hover your mouse over the link (without clicking!) to see the actual destination. Does it match the apparent sender?
Impersonation (Vishing/Pretexting):
Vishing (Voice Phishing): Someone calling claiming to be from IT support, HR, a bank, or a vendor, asking for your password, OTP, or other sensitive information over the phone.
Pretexting: A criminal creates a fabricated scenario (e.g., "I'm from our external audit firm, I need your login to review our system") to extract information.
Your Action!: Always verify the identity of unexpected callers or emailers. If someone calls claiming to be from IT, tell them you'll call them back using the official, known IT helpdesk number, not a number they provide.
Tailgating/Piggybacking:
An unauthorized person following an authorized person through a secure access point (e.g., holding a door open behind you, slipping in while you badge in).
Your Action!: Always ensure proper access control measures (e.g., closing doors behind you, challenging unknown individuals in restricted areas, ensuring visitors have badges).
Baiting:
Leaving infected USB drives or other enticing devices (e.g., a "free company gift" USB) in public places (e.g., parking lots, lobbies) hoping someone will pick them up and plug them into their computer out of curiosity.
Your Action!: Never plug unknown USB drives or external devices into your company laptop or desktop. Turn them over to IT if found.
How to Be Vigilant (Your Daily Checklist - Think Before You Click/Act!)
STOP, LOOK, THINK! This is your golden rule. Before clicking any link, opening an attachment, or responding to an unusual request, pause for a moment.
Verify the Sender: Always check the full email address, not just the display name. Does it match the sender? Is it from a legitimate domain (e.g., ourcompany.com, not ourcompony.biz)?
Hover Before You Click: Hover your mouse over links (without clicking!) to see the actual URL in the bottom-left corner of your browser. Does it look legitimate? If it looks suspicious, don't click!
Beware of Urgency & Threats: Cybercriminals often create a false sense of panic or urgency to bypass critical thinking. Take a moment to evaluate the request calmly. Is it truly urgent?
Be Suspicious of Unexpected Requests: Especially for login credentials, financial information, or gift cards, even if they appear to come from internal sources. Always verify via a different trusted channel.
Report, Don't Delete: If you suspect a phishing email, a suspicious call, or any unusual activity, do not delete it or try to handle it yourself. Report it to [Designated Security/IT Team/Hotline] immediately. Your report helps protect everyone.
Guard Physical Access: Protect your workspace, devices, and sensitive documents. Don't let unauthorized persons tailgate you into secure areas. Challenge unknown individuals.
Use Official Channels: If an unusual request comes via email, verify it using a different, known communication channel (e.g., call the person on their known work number from the company directory, don't reply to the suspicious email).
Slide 10: Incident Response: What to Do in Case of a Security Incident
A rapid and coordinated response is critical. Your immediate action can prevent a small incident from becoming a major crisis.
What is a Security Incident?
Any event that compromises the Confidentiality, Integrity, or Availability (CIA) of our information systems or data.
Think: Anything that seems "off" or indicates something bad has happened to our data or systems.
Examples:
Your computer behaving unusually (slow, pop-ups, files disappearing).
You or a colleague accidentally clicked on a phishing email link or opened a suspicious attachment.
You notice unauthorized access to your account or files.
Your company laptop, mobile phone, or a USB drive containing company data is lost or stolen.
You observe suspicious network activity (e.g., unusually high internet traffic).
Accidental disclosure of confidential data (e.g., emailing sensitive information to the wrong external person).
You receive a message or screen demanding ransom to unlock your files/computer.
