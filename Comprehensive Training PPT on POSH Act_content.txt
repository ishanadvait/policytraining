Slide 2: Introduction - Why POSH Matters
What is the POSH Act?
The Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013, commonly known as the POSH Act.
A landmark legislation in India, stemming from the Vishaka Guidelines laid down by the Supreme Court in 1997.
Objective: To provide protection against sexual harassment of women at the workplace and for the prevention and redressal of complaints of sexual harassment.
Why This Training Matters to You
Legal Compliance: Mandatory for all organizations with 10 or more employees to adhere to the Act. Non-compliance leads to penalties.
Ethical Responsibility: Our commitment to fostering a safe, respectful, and inclusive workplace culture where everyone feels valued and protected.
Individual Rights & Responsibilities: Understanding your rights and duties under the Act, whether as an employee, manager, or member of the Internal Complaints Committee (ICC).
Productivity & Morale: A safe workplace enhances employee morale, trust, and productivity, contributing to overall organizational success.
Training Objectives
To understand the definition and forms of sexual harassment as per the POSH Act.
To know your rights and responsibilities under the Act.
To learn about the Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) and its role.
To understand the complaint and inquiry process.
To contribute to creating a safe and respectful workplace for all.
Slide 3: Understanding Sexual Harassment: Definition & Forms
Definition of Sexual Harassment (Section 2(n))
Sexual harassment includes any unwelcome act or behavior (whether directly or by implication) such as:
Physical contact and advances.
A demand or request for sexual favors.
Making sexually colored remarks.
Showing pornography.
Any other unwelcome physical, verbal or non-verbal conduct of sexual nature.
Key Elements: "Unwelcome" and "Sexual Nature"
Unwelcome: The core element. It means the conduct is unsolicited and offensive to the recipient. Consent is absent, or if initially present, is later withdrawn. The perception of the recipient is crucial.
Sexual Nature: The conduct must have a sexual connotation or implication.
Forms of Sexual Harassment
Sexual harassment can take various forms, including:
Verbal Harassment:
Offensive jokes or comments with sexual undertones.
Sexually suggestive remarks, innuendos, or teasing.
Spreading rumors about someone's sex life.
Making derogatory or condescending comments based on gender.
Unwelcome compliments about appearance.
Demands or requests for sexual favors (explicit or implicit).
Non-Verbal Harassment:
Staring or leering.
Displaying sexually suggestive objects, pictures, cartoons, or posters.
Sexual gestures or body language.
Whistling or catcalling.
Sending unwanted explicit emails, messages, or social media content.
Online harassment (cyber-sexual harassment).
Physical Harassment:
Unwelcome touching, patting, pinching, brushing against.
Blocking a person's path.
Assault or molestation.
Physical advances or gestures.
Implied/Explicit Promise/Threat related to employment:
Quid Pro Quo Harassment: Directly or indirectly threatening or promising benefits in exchange for sexual favors (e.g., "If you do this for me, you'll get that promotion," or "If you don't, you'll lose your job/opportunity").
Implied or explicit threat about present or future employment status.
Implied or explicit threat about interfering with work or creating a hostile environment.
Understanding "Hostile Work Environment"
Occurs when unwelcome conduct of a sexual nature is severe or pervasive enough to create an intimidating, hostile, or offensive working environment.
It doesn't necessarily involve a direct demand for sexual favors but impacts the person's ability to work.
Examples: Continuous sexist jokes, display of inappropriate material, constant unwelcome comments, gender-based bullying creating a climate of discomfort.
Slide 4: The POSH Act, 2013: Key Provisions & Objectives
Objectives of the Act
Prevention: To prevent acts of sexual harassment.
Prohibition: To prohibit any acts of sexual harassment.
Redressal: To provide a mechanism for redressal of complaints related to sexual harassment.
Key Provisions of the Act
Mandatory Policy: Requires every employer to formulate and disseminate an internal policy on sexual harassment.
Internal Complaints Committee (ICC): Mandates the constitution of an ICC in every organization with 10 or more employees.
Local Complaints Committee (LCC): Mandates the constitution of an LCC at the district level for organizations with less than 10 employees or where the complaint is against the employer directly.
Complaint Mechanism: Outlines a clear process for filing and addressing complaints.
Inquiry Process: Specifies the procedure for inquiry, ensuring natural justice.
Interim Relief: Provisions for interim measures during the pendency of inquiry (e.g., transfer of parties, leave for aggrieved woman).
Conciliation: Option for conciliation (non-monetary) before inquiry.
Penalties: Prescribes penalties for non-compliance by employer and for malicious complaints.
Employer Responsibilities: Clearly defines the duties of the employer in ensuring a safe workplace.
What Constitutes a "Workplace"? (Section 2(o))
The definition of "workplace" under the POSH Act is very broad and inclusive. It includes:
Any department, organization, undertaking, establishment, enterprise, institution, office, branch or unit which is established, owned, controlled or wholly or substantially financed by funds provided by the appropriate Government or a local authority or a Government company or a corporation or a co-operative society.
Private sector organization or a private venture, undertaking, enterprise, institution, establishment, society, trust, non-governmental organization, unit or service provider carrying on commercial, professional, vocational, educational, entertainment, industrial, health services or financial activities.
Any place visited by the employee arising out of or during the course of employment, including:
Transportation provided by the employer for commuting to and from the workplace.
Places visited for official purposes (e.g., client meetings, conferences, training sessions, company events, off-site locations).
Even virtual workplaces or communications if related to work (e.g., official emails, video calls, team chats).
Slide 5: Applicability of the Act: Who is Covered?
Who is Protected? "Aggrieved Woman" (Section 2(a))
The Act protects any woman, of any age, whether employed or not, who is subjected to any act of sexual harassment at any workplace.
This includes:
Regular, temporary, ad-hoc, contractual, daily wage employees.
Workers, probationers, trainees, apprentices.
Interns.
Volunteers.
Students.
Clients or customers visiting the workplace.
Domestic workers.
Anyone entering the workplace.
Who Can Be a Respondent?
The person against whom the complaint of sexual harassment has been made is called the "Respondent."
A respondent can be:
Any employee (senior, peer, junior).
A manager or supervisor.
A director or board member.
A client, vendor, or customer.
A temporary staff, consultant, intern, or visitor.
Any person who commits an act of sexual harassment at the workplace.
Applicability Threshold
The Act mandates the constitution of an Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) in every organization having 10 or more employees.
If an organization has fewer than 10 employees, or if the complaint is against the employer himself, the aggrieved woman can file a complaint with the Local Complaints Committee (LCC) constituted by the District Officer.
Slide 6: Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) / Local Complaints Committee (LCC)
Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) - Mandatory for 10+ Employees
Composition (Section 4):
Presiding Officer: A woman employed at a senior level in the organization.
Minimum Two Members: From amongst employees, preferably committed to the cause of women or who have legal knowledge/experience in social work.
One External Member: From NGOs or associations committed to the cause of women or a person familiar with issues relating to sexual harassment.
Minimum 4 members, with at least half (50%) being women.
Term: Members hold office for a period not exceeding 3 years.
Quorum: Minimum 3 members, including the Presiding Officer/Chairperson.
Independence: Members are expected to act impartially and without bias.
Role and Functions of the ICC
Receive Complaints: Receive and acknowledge complaints of sexual harassment.
Conduct Inquiry: Conduct a fair, impartial, and timely inquiry into complaints.
Recommend Action: Recommend appropriate actions to the employer based on inquiry findings (e.g., disciplinary action, compensation).
Conciliation: Facilitate conciliation if requested by the aggrieved woman (non-monetary settlement only).
Awareness & Sensitization: Assist the employer in organizing workshops and awareness programs.
Annual Report: Submit an annual report to the District Officer and the employer.
Local Complaints Committee (LCC) - District Level
Constitution: Formed by the District Officer in every district (Section 6).
Composition: Includes a Chairperson, members from NGOs, a woman employee working in a block, taluka or tehsil, and the Social Welfare Officer of the district.
Jurisdiction: Handles complaints:
From establishments with fewer than 10 employees.
Where the complaint is against the employer themselves.
From the unorganized sector.
Functions: Similar to ICC, tailored to their jurisdiction.
Slide 7: Complaint Mechanism: How to File a Complaint
Who Can File a Complaint?
The Aggrieved Woman: The woman who has been subjected to sexual harassment.
Incapacity: If the aggrieved woman is unable to file a complaint (e.g., physical/mental incapacity, death), her legal heir or any other person as prescribed can file it on her behalf.
To Whom to File a Complaint?
Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) of your organization.
Local Complaints Committee (LCC) if the organization has less than 10 employees or the complaint is against the employer directly.
Time Limit for Filing a Complaint (Section 9)
The complaint must be filed within 3 months from the date of the incident.
In case of a series of incidents, within 3 months from the date of the last incident.
The ICC/LCC may extend the time limit for another 3 months if it is satisfied that the circumstances prevented the woman from filing the complaint within the initial period, for reasons to be recorded in writing.
What Should a Complaint Include?
The complaint should be in writing.
Ideally, it should include:
Name and contact details of the aggrieved woman.
Name of the respondent.
Date(s) and time(s) of the incident(s).
Description of the incident(s) with specific details.
Names of any witnesses (if applicable).
Supporting documents or evidence (if any, e.g., emails, messages, photos).
Desired redressal/action (optional, but can be helpful).
Number of copies: Six copies of the complaint along with supporting documents and names/addresses of parties/witnesses must be submitted.
Confidentiality
The identity of the aggrieved woman, respondent, and witnesses, as well as the details of the inquiry, must be kept confidential (Section 16).
Publication of identity or information could lead to a penalty.
Slide 8: Inquiry Process: Steps & Timelines
Step 1: Receiving the Complaint (Within 7 working days)
ICC/LCC receives the written complaint.
If the complaint is not in writing, the Presiding Officer/Chairperson must provide all reasonable assistance to the aggrieved woman to do so.
ICC/LCC will provide a copy of the complaint to the respondent within 7 working days.
Step 2: Response from Respondent (Within 10 working days)
The respondent has 10 working days to submit their written response to the complaint, along with any supporting documents or names of witnesses.
Step 3: Conciliation (Optional - Section 10)
Before commencing inquiry, the ICC/LCC may, at the request of the aggrieved woman, attempt conciliation.
Important: Conciliation should not involve any monetary settlement.
If conciliation is successful, the ICC/LCC records the settlement and forwards it to the employer for implementation. No further inquiry is conducted.
Step 4: Inquiry Proceedings (Within 90 days - Section 11)
If conciliation fails or is not opted for, the ICC/LCC proceeds with the inquiry.
The inquiry must be conducted in accordance with the principles of natural justice. This means:
Both parties (aggrieved woman and respondent) must be given a fair opportunity to be heard.
They can present their case, examine witnesses, and produce relevant documents.
They have the right to cross-examine witnesses presented by the other party.
No party can bring a legal practitioner to represent them in the inquiry proceedings.
The inquiry must be completed within a period of 90 days from the date of receiving the complaint.
The ICC/LCC has powers similar to a Civil Court for summoning witnesses and producing documents.
Step 5: Submission of Inquiry Report (Within 10 days of inquiry completion - Section 13)
Upon completion of the inquiry, the ICC/LCC prepares an Inquiry Report.
The report contains findings and recommendations.
Findings:
If allegations are proven: recommends action against the respondent.
If allegations are not proven: recommends no action.
If the complaint is found to be malicious or false: recommends action against the aggrieved woman (with specific safeguards).
The ICC/LCC submits the report to the employer (or District Officer) within 10 days of completing the inquiry.
Step 6: Employer's Action (Within 60 days - Section 13)
The employer (or District Officer) must act on the recommendations of the ICC/LCC within 60 days of receiving the report.
Actions can include:
Implementing disciplinary action (e.g., warning, apology, withholding promotion/increment, termination).
Directing compensation to the aggrieved woman.
Other appropriate measures.
If the employer disagrees with the ICC/LCC's findings, they must record reasons for their disagreement.
Interim Relief to Aggrieved Woman (Section 12)
During the pendency of the inquiry, at the request of the aggrieved woman, the ICC/LCC may recommend to the employer:
Transfer the aggrieved woman or the respondent to any other workplace.
Grant leave to the aggrieved woman up to 3 months (in addition to her regular leave entitlement).
Restrain the respondent from reporting on the work performance of, or evaluating, or supervising the aggrieved woman.
Any other relief as deemed appropriate.
Slide 9: Roles & Responsibilities
Employer's Responsibilities (Section 19)
The POSH Act places significant responsibility on the employer to ensure a safe workplace:
Constitute ICC: Ensure formation of an ICC with the correct composition and adequate training.
Formulate Policy: Develop and widely disseminate an anti-sexual harassment policy.
Provide a Safe Working Environment: Take all necessary steps to prevent sexual harassment.
Organize Awareness Programs: Conduct regular workshops and sensitization programs for all employees and ICC members.
Display Penal Consequences: Prominently display the penal consequences of sexual harassment and the order constituting the ICC.
Provide Assistance: Extend all necessary facilities to the ICC/LCC for conducting inquiries and enforcing recommendations.
Act on Recommendations: Implement the recommendations of the ICC/LCC within the stipulated timeframe.
Monitor & Report: Submit an annual report to the District Officer and ensure compliance.
Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) Responsibilities
Maintain strict confidentiality throughout the process.
Conduct fair, impartial, and timely inquiries based on principles of natural justice.
Provide a safe and supportive environment for complainants.
Act within the powers granted by the Act.
Submit accurate and timely inquiry reports.
Educate employees and assist the employer in prevention.
Employee's Responsibilities
Understand and Adhere: Be aware of and adhere to the organization's POSH policy.
Report Concerns: Promptly report any incidents of sexual harassment witnessed or experienced.
Cooperate with Inquiry: Cooperate fully and honestly with the ICC/LCC during any inquiry.
Respect Confidentiality: Maintain confidentiality of proceedings.
Promote a Respectful Culture: Contribute to a workplace culture free from harassment by treating all colleagues with dignity and respect.
Avoid Retaliation: Do not retaliate against a complainant or a witness.
Slide 10: Consequences of Sexual Harassment
Consequences for the Respondent (If Allegations are Proven)
Disciplinary Actions (Employer Discretion):
Written apology.
Warning, reprimand, or censure.
Withholding of promotion, increment, or pay raise.
Termination from service (for severe cases).
Transfer.
Community service or counseling.
Deduction from salary/wages to pay compensation to the aggrieved woman.
Criminal Charges: In severe cases (e.g., molestation, assault), separate criminal proceedings can be initiated under the Indian Penal Code (IPC) alongside the POSH inquiry.
Reputational Damage: Severe damage to personal and professional reputation, affecting future career prospects.
Legal Costs: Potential costs if criminal or civil cases are pursued.
Consequences for the Employer (For Non-Compliance)
Financial Penalties (Section 26):
Initial non-compliance (e.g., failure to constitute ICC, not acting on recommendations): Fine up to INR 50,000.
Repeated non-compliance: Fine can be doubled, and the employer's business license/registration may be cancelled.
Reputational Damage: Negative publicity, loss of public trust, difficulty in attracting and retaining talent.
Legal Action: Potential lawsuits from aggrieved parties.
Impact on Morale: A workplace seen as unsafe can severely impact employee morale and productivity.
Consequences for Malicious Complaints or False Evidence (Section 14)
The Act provides safeguards against misuse. If the ICC/LCC concludes that the complaint was made maliciously or with false intent, or that false evidence was provided knowingly, it may recommend action against the complainant.
Safeguards: This provision is not meant to deter genuine complaints, but to prevent false accusations. A mere inability to substantiate a complaint is not considered malicious intent.
Action: Disciplinary action against the complainant (similar to that against a respondent) can be recommended, but this should be exercised with extreme caution and only when malicious intent is clearly established.
Impact on Workplace Culture
Sexual harassment creates a hostile, intimidating, and offensive environment.
Decreases employee morale, trust, and productivity.
Increases absenteeism and employee turnover.
Damages the organization's brand and reputation.
Undermines diversity and inclusion efforts.
Slide 11: Prevention & Prohibition Measures
Proactive Measures by the Organization
Strong Leadership Commitment (Tone from the Top): Management must visibly champion a zero-tolerance policy towards sexual harassment and clearly communicate its commitment to a safe workplace.
Clear & Comprehensive POSH Policy: Ensure the policy is easily accessible, clearly defines sexual harassment, outlines the complaint and redressal mechanism, and details consequences.
What a Desirable Policy Includes:
Zero-Tolerance Statement: Explicit commitment to preventing and addressing sexual harassment.
Definition of Sexual Harassment: Clear, easy-to-understand explanation of all forms, including examples.
Scope & Applicability: Who is covered (employees, interns, visitors, third parties) and what constitutes the 'workplace' (physical, virtual, off-site events).
ICC Details: Names, contact information, and roles of ICC members.
Complaint Procedure: Step-by-step guide on how to file a complaint, timelines, and required documentation.
Inquiry Process: Explanation of the inquiry stages, principles of natural justice, and expected timelines.
Interim Relief: Details on available interim measures for the aggrieved woman during inquiry.
Conciliation Process: How and when conciliation can be opted for (non-monetary).
Confidentiality Clause: Assurance that identities and details will be protected.
Non-Retaliation Clause: Strong statement against any form of retaliation towards complainants or witnesses.
Consequences of Proven Harassment: Clear outline of disciplinary actions against respondents.
Consequences of Malicious Complaints: Safeguards and actions against false complaints.
Roles & Responsibilities: Clarifies duties of employer, ICC, and all employees.
Awareness & Training: Commitment to regular sensitization programs.
Annual Reporting: Information on the submission of the annual report.
Regular Awareness & Sensitization Workshops:
For all employees: To educate them on what constitutes harassment, how to prevent it, and how to report.
For ICC members: To equip them with the necessary skills for inquiry, conciliation, and maintaining confidentiality.
Conduct these periodically (e.g., annually) and for new joinees.
Prominent Display: Display the POSH policy, penal consequences, and ICC contact details at conspicuous places in the workplace.
Internal Communication: Regularly communicate the importance of a respectful workplace through emails, intranet, posters, and team meetings.
Anonymous Feedback Channels: Consider providing mechanisms for anonymous feedback to gauge workplace culture and potential issues.
Gender Sensitization & Equity: Promote gender equality and sensitize employees to gender-related issues beyond harassment, fostering an inclusive environment.
Support for Aggrieved Persons: Provide access to counseling services or support mechanisms for those who have experienced harassment.
Your Role in Prevention and Prohibition
Be Aware: Understand what constitutes sexual harassment and recognize red flags.
Speak Up (Directly or Indirectly):
If comfortable, clearly and firmly communicate to the harasser that their behavior is unwelcome.
Report incidents, whether you are the aggrieved person or a witness.
Be an Ally/Bystander Intervention: If you witness harassment, consider intervening safely (e.g., distracting, confronting, or reporting).
Treat Everyone with Respect: Be mindful of your words, actions, and non-verbal cues. Ensure your behavior is always professional and respectful.
Respect Personal Space: Be conscious of physical boundaries.
Avoid Stereotypes & Jokes: Refrain from making or laughing at sexist, homophobic, or derogatory jokes.
Maintain Professionalism Online: Apply the same standards of conduct to online communication (emails, chat groups, social media) related to work.
Ask for Consent: Always seek explicit consent if you intend to engage in any physical contact or personal conversation.
Lead by Example: Managers and leaders must role model appropriate behavior and ensure their teams understand and comply with the policy.
