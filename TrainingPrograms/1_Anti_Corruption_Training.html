The Indian Legal Landscape

Key anti-corruption laws governing business in India

Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 (PCA)

üìú The Cornerstone of Indian Anti-Corruption Law

Primary legislation in India to combat corruption, specifically targeting public servants.

Broad Definition of "Public Servant" (Section 2(c))

The PCA defines a public servant very broadly, including:

  • Any person in the service or pay of the Government (Central or State)
  • Employees of local authorities, government corporations, or aided bodies
  • Judges, arbitrators, and court-authorized persons
  • Office bearers of registered co-operative societies receiving government aid
  • Employees of educational/scientific institutions receiving substantial government assistance

Key Offenses Under PCA

Public servant taking gratification for performing official acts

¬ß8
Section 8

Taking gratification to influence public servant by corrupt means

¬ß9
Section 9

Bribing a public servant by a commercial organization

¬ß10
Section 10

Personal liability of persons in charge of commercial organizations

2018 Amendments - Critical Changes

‚ö° Major 2018 Updates
  • Criminalized Bribe-Giving: Both givers and takers are now equally liable
  • Prior Sanction Required: Section 17A requires government approval for investigations
  • Enhanced Penalties: Minimum 3 years to maximum 7 years imprisonment
  • "Adequate Procedures" Defense: Companies can defend if they had robust compliance programs
  • Faster Trials: Courts must complete trials within 2-4 years

Penalties Under PCA

  • Rigorous imprisonment: 3-7 years depending on offense
  • Substantial fines linked to gratification amount
  • Confiscation/forfeiture of property acquired through corruption

International Anti-Bribery Laws

Global laws with significant impact on Indian business

Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) - USA

üá∫üá∏ Enacted in 1977

Prevents US companies and individuals from bribing foreign government officials to obtain or retain business.

Who Does FCPA Apply To?

  • US Issuers: Any company with securities listed on US stock exchange
  • US Domestic Concerns: Any US citizen, national, resident, or business entity
  • Foreign Persons/Entities: Non-US entities committing acts while in US territory
‚ö†Ô∏è Broad Jurisdictional Reach

Even a single email through US servers, a phone call routed through US networks, or a bank transaction through a US institution can establish FCPA jurisdiction.

Key FCPA Components

üö´
Anti-Bribery Provisions

Criminalizes corrupt payments of "anything of value" to foreign officials

üìä
Accounting Provisions

Requires accurate books/records and robust internal accounting controls

UK Bribery Act, 2010

üá¨üáß One of the Strictest Globally

Covers bribery in both public and private sectors with broad extraterritorial reach.

Key Offenses

  • Section 1: Bribing another person
  • Section 2: Being bribed
  • Section 6: Bribery of foreign public officials
  • Section 7: Failure of commercial organizations to prevent bribery (strict liability)
üí° Impact on Indian Companies
  • Indian companies with UK operations, subsidiaries, or branches
  • Indian companies with UK-based agents or intermediaries
  • Any entity with a "business presence" in the UK

Defining Bribery and Corruption in Practice

Understanding what constitutes prohibited conduct

What Constitutes an "Undue Advantage" or Bribe?

A bribe is anything of value offered, promised, or given to improperly influence an action or decision. The concept is extremely broad:

üíµ
Direct Payments

Cash, bank transfers, loans at below-market rates, gift cards

üéÅ
Gifts & Hospitality

Excessive/lavish gifts, expensive meals, travel, entertainment

üí≥
Facilitation Payments

Small payments to expedite routine government actions

üîÑ
Kickbacks

Returning a portion of payment, disguised as "commissions"

üë®‚Äçüë©‚Äçüëß
Nepotism/Cronyism

Unfairly favoring family members or associates

üèõÔ∏è
Political Contributions

Donations intended to improperly influence officials

Key Elements of a Bribery Offense

üìã An act constitutes bribery if there is:
  1. An Offer, Promise, or Grant: Proposing, agreeing, or giving something of value
  2. Of an Undue Advantage: Money, gift, favor, or benefit regardless of size
  3. To Induce or Reward: Corrupt intent to influence action
  4. Improper Performance: Action relates to duty performed improperly
  5. Direct or Indirect: Through self or intermediaries

Red Flags and High-Risk Areas

Warning signs that require heightened scrutiny

üö© What are Red Flags?

Warning signs that indicate a higher risk of corruption or bribery. They are not proof of wrongdoing but signal the need for heightened scrutiny and due diligence.

Interacting with Public Officials

  • Requests for "speed money" or unofficial payments
  • Unusual requests for donations or sponsorship linked to officials
  • Officials pushing for a specific third-party vendor without justification
  • Lack of transparency in government processes
  • High-value gifts or hospitality requests

Third-Party Relationships

‚ö†Ô∏è
Questionable Reputation

Parties known for unethical practices or past allegations

üí∞
Unusual Payment Terms

Cash, offshore accounts, different names, shell companies

üìà
Disproportionate Fees

Excessive commissions inconsistent with market rates

üìù
Vague Scope of Work

No written contract or inability to articulate services

Gifts, Hospitality, and Entertainment

  • Frequent or lavish gifts exceeding policy limits
  • Travel/accommodation for officials or their families
  • Lack of clear approval process
  • Gifts made in secret or undeclared

Procurement and Tendering

  • Lack of competitive bidding without justification
  • Specifications tailored to favor specific bidders
  • Unexplained contract term changes post-tender
  • Sudden inclusion of new third parties late in process

Real-World Case Studies

Learning from actual enforcement actions

üìñ Cognizant (India)

A US-based IT services firm paid over $25 million in penalties to the SEC for bribing Indian officials to obtain building permits, violating the FCPA.

Lesson: Need for robust internal controls and due diligence, even for routine matters.

üìñ Oracle (India)

US tech giant paid $23 million to the SEC for FCPA violations when employees of its Indian subsidiary created "slush funds" with distributors for unauthorized payments.

Lesson: Risks in sales and channel partner relationships require vigilance.

üìñ CDM Smith (India)

US engineering firm faced $34 million disgorgement after employees bribed NHAI officials for contracts, disguising bribes as "Allowable Business Expenditure."

Lesson: Accounting fraud risks and the importance of transparent financial records.
