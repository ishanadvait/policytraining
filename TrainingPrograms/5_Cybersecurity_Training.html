Information Security vs Cybersecurity

Understanding the relationship and differences

üìÑ
Information Security (InfoSec)

The practice of protecting all forms of information (digital and non-digital). Includes policies, procedures, and controls for protecting the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information assets.

üíª
Cybersecurity

A subset of InfoSec focused specifically on protecting digital information, computer systems, networks, and devices from cyber threats. Often more technical and technology-focused.

üîç The Relationship

Cybersecurity is part of the broader Information Security discipline. While cybersecurity focuses on digital threats, InfoSec also covers physical security of documents, verbal communications, and organizational policies.

Key Security Areas

  • Network Security: Protecting network infrastructure from unauthorized access
  • Application Security: Securing software applications from vulnerabilities
  • Endpoint Security: Protecting devices (laptops, mobiles, servers)
  • Cloud Security: Securing data and applications in cloud environments
  • Data Security: Protecting data at rest, in transit, and in use
  • Operational Security: Day-to-day security practices and procedures

The CIA Triad

The three pillars of information security

üî∫ The Foundation of Information Security

The CIA Triad represents the three core objectives of information security. All security measures aim to protect one or more of these properties.

üîí
Confidentiality

Ensuring that information is accessible only to authorized individuals. Protection against unauthorized disclosure. Achieved through: access controls, encryption, data classification.

‚úì
Integrity

Ensuring that information is accurate and has not been tampered with. Protection against unauthorized modification. Achieved through: checksums, digital signatures, version control, audit trails.

‚ö°
Availability

Ensuring that information and systems are accessible when needed by authorized users. Protection against disruption. Achieved through: redundancy, backups, disaster recovery, DDoS protection.

Examples of CIA Violations

Principle Violation Example
Confidentiality Customer database leaked; employee shares password
Integrity Financial records altered; malware corrupts files
Availability Ransomware attack; DDoS takes website offline

Common Cyber Threats

Understanding the enemy

Malware (Malicious Software)

ü¶†
Viruses

Self-replicating code that attaches to legitimate programs. Spreads when infected file is executed.

üêõ
Worms

Self-replicating malware that spreads across networks without user action.

üé≠
Trojans

Malware disguised as legitimate software. Creates backdoors for attackers.

üí∞
Ransomware

Encrypts files and demands ransom for decryption. Major threat to organizations.

üëÅÔ∏è
Spyware

Secretly monitors user activity and collects data (keyloggers, screen capture).

üì¢
Adware

Displays unwanted ads. Can track browsing habits and slow systems.

Ransomware - A Growing Threat

üí∞ Ransomware Attack Cycle
  1. Infection: Usually through phishing email or compromised website
  2. Encryption: Files are encrypted with strong encryption
  3. Ransom Demand: Attacker demands payment (usually cryptocurrency)
  4. Data Exfiltration: Modern ransomware also steals data before encrypting

Prevention: Regular backups, patch management, email filtering, user awareness

Other Threats

  • DDoS Attacks: Overwhelm systems with traffic, causing unavailability
  • Man-in-the-Middle (MitM): Intercepting communications between two parties
  • SQL Injection: Exploiting vulnerabilities in web applications
  • Zero-Day Exploits: Attacks on unknown vulnerabilities before patches exist
  • Insider Threats: Malicious or negligent employees

Phishing & Social Engineering

The human element of hacking

What is Social Engineering?

üé≠ Manipulating Humans, Not Machines

Social engineering is the art of psychologically manipulating people into performing actions or divulging confidential information. It exploits human trust, curiosity, fear, and helpfulness.

Types of Phishing

üìß
Email Phishing

Mass emails impersonating trusted entities (banks, IT support, HR) with malicious links or attachments.

üéØ
Spear Phishing

Targeted attacks on specific individuals using personalized information gathered from social media.

üêã
Whaling

Spear phishing targeting high-level executives (CEOs, CFOs) for high-value fraud.

üì±
Smishing

Phishing via SMS text messages.

üìû
Vishing

Phishing via voice calls (e.g., fake bank/tech support calls).

üîó
Clone Phishing

Cloning a legitimate email and replacing links with malicious ones.

How to Spot a Phishing Email

üö© Red Flags
  • Suspicious Sender: Check the actual email address, not just the display name
  • Urgency/Threats: "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours!"
  • Generic Greeting: "Dear Customer" instead of your name
  • Grammar/Spelling Errors: Professional organizations proofread
  • Suspicious Links: Hover over links to see actual URL before clicking
  • Unexpected Attachments: Never open attachments you weren't expecting
  • Too Good to Be True: Lottery wins, unexpected refunds
  • Request for Sensitive Info: Passwords, OTPs, bank details

Other Social Engineering Techniques

  • Pretexting: Creating a fabricated scenario to extract information
  • Baiting: Offering something enticing (free USB, download) that is malicious
  • Tailgating: Following authorized personnel into secure areas
  • Quid Pro Quo: Offering fake tech support in exchange for login credentials

Password & Access Security

Your first line of defense

Creating Strong Passwords

‚úÖ Strong Password Guidelines
  • Length: At least 12-16 characters (longer is better)
  • Complexity: Mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and special characters
  • Unique: Different password for each account (NEVER reuse passwords)
  • No Personal Info: Avoid names, birthdays, pet names, dictionary words
  • Passphrase: Consider a memorable phrase like "MyDog@te3Pizz@s!"

Password DON'Ts

‚ùå Never Do These
  • Never share your password with anyone (including IT support)
  • Never write passwords on sticky notes or store in plain text
  • Never use the same password for work and personal accounts
  • Never use simple patterns (123456, password, qwerty)
  • Never send passwords via email or messaging apps
  • Never enter passwords on unfamiliar devices or networks

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

üî¢
Something You Know

Password, PIN, security question

üì±
Something You Have

Phone (OTP), hardware token, smart card

üëÜ
Something You Are

Fingerprint, facial recognition, voice

üí° Enable MFA Everywhere

MFA significantly increases security. Even if a password is compromised, attackers cannot access the account without the second factor. Enable MFA on all accounts that support it!

Access Control Principles

  • Least Privilege: Only grant access necessary for the job function
  • Need-to-Know: Access information only when required for work
  • Segregation of Duties: Critical tasks require multiple approvers
  • Regular Review: Periodically review and revoke unnecessary access

Data Protection Best Practices

Handling data securely

Data Classification

üî¥
Confidential / Restricted

Highly sensitive (trade secrets, financial data, PII, customer data). Strictest controls.

üü°
Internal

Internal use only. Not for public disclosure but less sensitive.

üü¢
Public

Can be freely shared (marketing materials, published content).

Encryption

üîê Scrambling Data for Protection
  • Data at Rest: Encrypt hard drives, databases, backups
  • Data in Transit: Use HTTPS, VPN, TLS/SSL for communications
  • Email Encryption: Encrypt sensitive email attachments
  • Full Disk Encryption: Protect laptops and mobile devices

Secure Data Disposal

üóëÔ∏è Don't Just Delete - Destroy
  • Physical Documents: Shred confidential papers (cross-cut shredder)
  • Digital Data: Use secure deletion tools (not just delete or format)
  • Storage Devices: Degauss or physically destroy before disposal
  • Old Equipment: Ensure data is wiped before recycling/disposing

Safe Internet & Email Practices

  • Verify email senders before clicking links or opening attachments
  • Never download software from untrusted sources
  • Check for HTTPS before entering sensitive information on websites
  • Be cautious on public Wi-Fi ‚Äì use VPN for sensitive work
  • Don't overshare on social media (can be used for social engineering)

Incident Response

When things go wrong

What is a Security Incident?

Any event that potentially compromises the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of information or systems, including:

  • Malware infection
  • Suspected phishing or social engineering
  • Unauthorized access attempts
  • Lost or stolen devices
  • Data breach or data leak
  • Suspicious system behavior

Your Role in Incident Response

‚úÖ What to Do
  1. DON'T PANIC ‚Äì Stay calm and act quickly
  2. STOP: Disconnect from network if instructed (e.g., unplug Ethernet, disable Wi-Fi)
  3. DON'T DELETE: Preserve evidence ‚Äì do not delete files or emails
  4. REPORT IMMEDIATELY: Contact IT Security / Helpdesk without delay
  5. DOCUMENT: Note what happened, when, and what you observed
  6. COOPERATE: Assist IT/Security team with investigation
  7. DON'T INVESTIGATE: Leave forensics to experts
