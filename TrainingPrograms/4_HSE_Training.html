Understanding Health, Safety & Environment

Core concepts and definitions

The Three Pillars of HSE

‚ù§Ô∏è
Health

Focuses on protecting workers from occupational illnesses and diseases caused by workplace exposure. Includes physical health (ergonomics, hygiene, exposure to substances) and mental health (stress, burnout, psychosocial factors).

üõ°Ô∏è
Safety

Focuses on preventing accidents and injuries in the workplace. Involves identifying hazards, assessing risks, implementing controls, and promoting safe behaviors.

üåç
Environment

Focuses on minimizing the impact of our operations on the natural environment. Includes proper waste management, resource conservation, pollution prevention, and regulatory compliance.

Hazard vs. Risk

‚ö†Ô∏è Understanding the Difference
Hazard Risk
A source or situation with potential to cause harm (e.g., wet floor, live wire, chemical) The likelihood that harm will occur due to exposure to the hazard, combined with the severity of that harm
Example: A wet floor Example: The chance someone will slip, fall, and get injured

Hierarchy of Controls

üìä Most to Least Effective
  1. Elimination: Remove the hazard entirely (most effective)
  2. Substitution: Replace hazardous materials with safer alternatives
  3. Engineering Controls: Isolate or contain the hazard (guards, ventilation)
  4. Administrative Controls: Change work practices, training, signage
  5. PPE: Personal Protective Equipment (last line of defense)

Legal Framework in India

Key laws governing workplace safety and environment

The Factories Act, 1948

üè≠ Primary Occupational Safety Law

Applies to all premises where manufacturing is carried out with 10+ workers (with power) or 20+ workers (without power). Key provisions include:

  • Health provisions (cleanliness, ventilation, lighting, drinking water)
  • Safety provisions (fencing of machinery, safe working conditions)
  • Welfare provisions (first aid, canteens, rest rooms)
  • Regulation of working hours and employment of women/children

The Environment Protection Act, 1986

  • Umbrella legislation for environmental protection in India
  • Empowers Central Government to take measures for protecting and improving environment
  • Sets standards for emissions/discharges and restrictions on hazardous substances

Other Relevant Laws

üí®
Air (Prevention & Control of Pollution) Act, 1981

Controls air pollution from industrial emissions

üíß
Water (Prevention & Control of Pollution) Act, 1974

Prevents and controls water pollution

‚ò¢Ô∏è
Hazardous Waste Management Rules

Regulates handling, storage, and disposal of hazardous waste

‚ö°
Electricity Act, 2003

Safety provisions for electrical installations

DoT Guidelines (Telco Specific)

üì° Department of Telecommunications Guidelines

Specific safety guidelines for tower construction, maintenance work at heights, electromagnetic field (EMF) safety, and cable laying operations.

Finding and evaluating workplace hazards

HIRA Process

üìã Hazard Identification and Risk Assessment
  1. Identify Hazards: Walk through, review records, consult workers

Common Workplace Hazards

Recognizing dangers in your work environment

Physical Hazards

üîä
Noise

Loud machinery, equipment. Can cause hearing loss. Use ear protection.

üì≥
Vibration

Hand-arm or whole-body vibration from tools/vehicles. Can cause nerve damage.

üå°Ô∏è
Temperature Extremes

Heat stroke, hypothermia. Stay hydrated, take breaks, dress appropriately.

üí°
Poor Lighting

Can lead to eye strain and accidents. Ensure adequate illumination.

Electrical Hazards

‚ö° Electrical Safety
  • Shocks & Burns: Contact with live wires - can be fatal
  • Arc Flash: Explosive release of energy from electrical equipment
  • Fire: Faulty wiring, overloaded circuits
  • Prevention: LOTO (Lock Out Tag Out), proper grounding, regular inspections

Chemical Hazards

  • Toxic Substances: Can cause poisoning, organ damage
  • Corrosives: Can burn skin, eyes, respiratory system
  • Flammables: Risk of fire and explosion
  • Control: SDSs (Safety Data Sheets), proper storage, ventilation, PPE

Ergonomic Hazards

  • Poor workstation design leading to musculoskeletal disorders
  • Repetitive motions, awkward postures, prolonged sitting/standing
  • Prevention: Ergonomic assessments, adjustable furniture, regular breaks

Psychosocial Hazards

üß† Mental Health Matters
  • Work-related stress, excessive workload, long hours
  • Harassment, bullying, lack of support
  • Can lead to burnout, anxiety, depression
  • Support: EAP programs, open communication, work-life balance

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

Your last line of defense

‚ö†Ô∏è Remember

PPE is the last line of defense in the hierarchy of controls. It should be used in conjunction with other control measures, not as a substitute.

Types of PPE

ü™ñ
Head Protection

Hard hats protect against falling objects and head impacts

üëì
Eye & Face Protection

Safety glasses, goggles, face shields for chemical/particle hazards

üëÇ
Hearing Protection

Earplugs, earmuffs for high-noise environments

üò∑
Respiratory Protection

Masks, respirators for dust, fumes, gases

üß§
Hand Protection

Gloves appropriate for the hazard (chemical, cut, heat)

üë¢
Foot Protection

Safety shoes with toe protection, anti-slip soles

ü¶∫
High-Visibility Clothing

Reflective vests for visibility in traffic/low-light areas

ü™¢
Fall Protection

Harnesses, lanyards, lifelines for work at heights

Your PPE Responsibilities

‚úÖ Employee Duties
  • Wear it properly: As trained and directed
  • Inspect before use: Check for damage or defects
  • Maintain it: Clean, store, and care for PPE properly
  • Report issues: Report damaged or defective PPE immediately
  • Don't modify: Never alter or disable PPE

Emergency Preparedness

Being ready for the unexpected

Types of Emergencies

üî•
Fire

Activate alarm, evacuate, close doors, use fire extinguishers only if safe

üíß
Flood/Natural Disaster

Follow evacuation routes, move to higher ground

‚ò¢Ô∏è
Chemical Spill

Evacuate, contain if trained, alert authorities

üè•
Medical Emergency

Call for help, provide first aid if trained, do not move injured unless necessary

Fire Safety - RACE

üî• RACE Procedure
  • R - Rescue: Remove anyone in immediate danger (if safe to do so)
  • A - Alarm: Activate the fire alarm and call emergency services
  • C - Contain: Close doors and windows to contain the fire
  • E - Extinguish/Evacuate: Use extinguisher only if trained and safe; otherwise evacuate

Fire Extinguisher - PASS

üßØ PASS Technique
  • P - Pull: Pull the pin to unlock
  • A - Aim: Aim at the base of the fire
  • S - Squeeze: Squeeze the handle to release agent
  • S - Sweep: Sweep from side to side

Know Before an Emergency

  • Location of fire extinguishers, first aid kits, fire alarms
  • Evacuation routes and assembly points
  • Emergency contact numbers (security, fire, ambulance)
  • Names of trained first aiders and fire wardens

First Aid Basics

Immediate response can save lives

Golden Rules of First Aid

‚úÖ Remember These
  • Stay Calm: A calm responder is more effective
  • Assess the Scene: Ensure it's safe for you to approach
  • Call for Help: Alert trained first aiders or emergency services
  • Do No Further Harm: Don't move injured unless absolutely necessary
  • Reassure the Victim: Keep them calm and still

Common First Aid Situations

ü©π
Minor Cuts/Wounds

Clean with water, apply pressure to stop bleeding, cover with bandage

üî•
Burns

Cool under running water for 10-20 minutes, cover loosely, seek medical help for severe burns

ü´Å
Choking

Encourage coughing, perform back blows and abdominal thrusts if needed

‚ö°
Electric Shock

Do NOT touch victim until power is off, call emergency services, check breathing

First Aid Kit Contents

Standard workplace first aid kits should include:

  • Adhesive bandages (various sizes)
  • Sterile gauze pads and roller bandages
  • Triangular bandages (slings)
  • Antiseptic wipes
  • Disposable gloves
  • Scissors and tweezers
  • CPR mask
  • First aid manual

Incident Reporting

Report to prevent recurrence

What to Report

üö®
Accidents

Any event causing injury to persons or damage to property

‚ö†Ô∏è
Near Misses

Events that could have caused injury but didn't (equally important!)

üî¥
Unsafe Conditions

Hazards you observe (broken equipment, spills, blocked exits)

üü°
Unsafe Acts

Behaviors that could lead to accidents (not wearing PPE, horseplay)

Why Report Near Misses?

üí° Learning Opportunity

Near misses are "free lessons" - they indicate hazards that could cause future accidents. Reporting them allows us to take corrective action before someone gets hurt.

How to Report

  1. Immediately alert your supervisor
  2. Provide first aid if needed (for injuries)
  3. Secure the scene if there's ongoing danger
  4. Complete incident report form (paper or online)
  5. Cooperate with investigation
